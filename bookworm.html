What is Bookworm?

Bookworm is a reading platform that fosters habitual reading and connections with other readers. I took on this process to explore the UX design process, improve my design skills, and work on my responsive design skills.

The Problem

Reading is one of the most valuable activities we can do. Intellectually, it advances our knowledge and emotionally, it allows us to escape reality for some time. However, life can sometimes get in the way and limit our time and motivation to read. It can be difficult to focus wholeheartedly on reading and make a regular commitment to do so. So given these constraints, how can we create a platform that promotes commitment and motivation to reading, while making it an enjoyable experience?

The Solution

Bookworm, a reading platform, allows for individuals to set reading goals, connect with like-minded readers, and find books that pique their interests.

Empathize

User Survey

To get started with this project, I surveyed potential reading app users to attain an understanding of reading habits and/or stuggles of individuals.

Insights

From analyzing my survey responses, I recognize that people generally appear to like reading but do not have the time or focus to commit to it. In addition, people struggle to find books that interest them. However, the majority of those surveyed indicate that they do want to read more and are willing to try a platform that addresses these discrepancies.

Pain Points

I consolidated the survey insights into key categories of struggle for individuals in their reading experiences, which I would like to address in this project.

Usability Test #1

Do my designs work? Before moving onto high fidelity designing, I conducted a usability study among 3 people to learn their impressions of the Bookworm platform over mobile, tablet, and web interfaces.

The main goal of this study was to understand the usersâ€™ impressions of the BookWorm platform to iterate upon the platform.

User Tasks

1. Navigate to the first book on your current reads
2. Search a book using a suggested filter and start reading it
3. View a discussion and write a comment
4. View someoneâ€™s profile and add them as a friend
5. Add a book to your wishlist from recommended

Data Collected

I documented observations of how the users went through their given tasks, as well as their commentary and questions. I also collected each userâ€™s time on the task, task completion rate amongst the participants, and SEQ rating per task. The users also completed a survey providing feedback post-study.

Evaluations

I created an affinity diagram to organize observations and feedback that I obtained during the study as well as from the post study survey.

Overall, the participants were able to successfully go through most of the tasks. However, there are certainly notable areas of the app that were problematic for the users to navigate through. The main general issue I uncovered, based on participant feedback during and after each task, was where to find features of the app. For example, some of my participants were confused on whether they can find books in the recommended section of home, or if they would only be able to find it in the "search" page.

Iterate

As I created the high fidelity designs of Bookworm, I aimed to advance various features to provide to best experience of productivity and motivation for Bookworm users. Here are some notable iterations I created across the different screens that include some of the many changes I included, as well as addresses the feedback from my usability test.

Design System

I created a simple design system to organize the general UI of Bookworm high fidelity designs.

Goals Display

I created a simpler, but more visual display of a user's progress on their reads and challenges by designing a pie chart instead of a progress bar. This allows for a clearer display of the reader's progress, better indicating how the user has met or not met their goals, with minimal clutter of the chart and text.

Filtered Search

Instead of including the book filter options on the discover page above the displayed book categories, I decided to only display those search filters once the user clicks to search content. This would allow for the user to more easily view the suggested books content and have an easier method of searching if they are trying to search for more content.

Community Page Redesign

First, I renamed "Community" to "Connect" to make it more clear that there could be a variety of ways users can connect with others in reading, not only joining a group. Also, instead of landing directly on the friends page once the user navigates to this page, I created another subpage, Trending. This page allows for the user to get a mixture of community related content such as posts, notable groups, friend suggestions, etc, while also letting them view the navigation to other pages: Friends, Discussions and Book Clubs.

Settings

I first designed a general settings page for the app as a whole. I then designed simple individual pages for users to set notifications and reading preferences. The notifications are intended to allow users to create regular habits to read, and the reading preferences allow for users to indicate their preferred content so they can achieve reading recommendations that they are more likely to commit to reading.

Reflection

Wow, can't believe I'm done... or am I? The biggest thing that I got out of designing Bookworm is that I was able to explore the UX Process in more depth. In doing so I was able to gain more insight into not only the UX design process, but also the overall product development process, which allowed me to design Bookworm in a more user-centric and strategic fashion. From doing so, I gained a better understanding of the uportance of research - both user and market research - in developing any product. In addition, I learned to use a new design tool, Adobe XD! While I still have a lot to learn with using Adobe XD, I enjoyed exploring a new design tool, and hope to explore even more in the future.

I'm most definitely not done with Bookworm! While this is a good stopping point, I would like to conduct another usability test (or several more) and keep innovating new features to make reading a more enjoyable and productive experience. I intend to continually work on making Bookworm increasingly more user centric across a variety of devices.
